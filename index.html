The Algorithm Election Comes to Japan

A data-driven look at how two breakout parties—DPP and Sanseito—rose together in the polls, but only one dominated YouTube by turning outrage into attention

By Kensho Kuremoto (Lede 2025)

Japan’s politics are in disarray. On July 20, in a stunning rebuke of the ruling coalition, voters stripped the government of its upper house majority. In the charts that follow, the ruling parties are marked in red and pink.

This comes after Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba’s coalition lost control of the lower house last year—meaning, for the first time since the founding of the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) in 1955, Japan’s dominant party no longer controls either chamber of the Diet. But this isn’t a clean transfer of power. Japan’s multiparty system is fractious. Coalition-building is rare. Every faction is a minority. Hence the chaos.

Two Newcomers, One Digital Surge

Amid the political disarray, two upstart parties have made striking gains: the Democratic Party for the People (DPP) and Sanseito. The DPP grew from 9 to 22 seats; Sanseito surged from 2 to 15. Given that only half of the upper house seats were up for election, the scale of these gains is notable. In recent party support polls, both the DPP and Sanseito stand out—especially remarkable for parties founded less than five years ago.

A key force behind both parties' rise: social media. Platforms like X, TikTok, and especially YouTube helped connect them to a younger generation. The chart below shows vote choice by age. Younger voters lean toward the DPP or Sanseito. Support for the ruling coalition rises with age.

Not All Populists Are the Same

Though often grouped together as social-media-driven populist upstarts, DPP and Sanseito chart fundamentally different paths.

DPP, led by Yuichiro Tamaki (56), is a centrist party founded about five years ago under the slogan “Resolution rather than confrontation.” Tamaki, a prominent opposition figure in Japanese politics, has long used social media as a direct channel to voters, making the DPP one of the most visible political forces both online and offline.

Sanseito, by contrast, was built from the internet up. Led by Sohei Kamiya (47), a right-wing politician and former member of the ruling LDP, the party was founded by rallying supporters online and quickly gained traction through social media. In this election, it ran under the slogan “Japanese First,” promoting hardline stances on immigration, opposition to vaccines and globalization, and support for traditional gender roles—positions that have drawn comparisons to MAGA Republicans in the U.S., Germany’s AfD, and the UK’s Reform Party.
